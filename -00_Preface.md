## Why Stroke?

Every year, over **12 million people** worldwide suffer a stroke. Approximately 9 million of these are ischemic strokes—where a blood clot blocks a vessel in the brain.

Among these 9 million patients:
- About **4 million** will die or become permanently disabled
- About **2 million** will have another stroke within one year
- The average long-term care cost in the United States exceeds **$300,000 per patient**

This represents a massive unmet medical need.

But most surprisingly: **for 30 years, there has been only one effective drug for this condition**—tPA (tissue plasminogen activator).

Why?

---

## The tPA Dilemma

tPA received FDA approval in 1996 and remains the first (and only) drug proven effective for acute ischemic stroke.

But 30 years later, global tPA utilization remains below **5%**.

This isn't due to lack of patients, but rather because:

**1. The treatment window is too narrow**

tPA must be administered within **4.5 hours** of symptom onset. Beyond this window, it's not only ineffective but significantly increases the risk of intracranial hemorrhage (ICH).

The reality is that most stroke patients cannot reach a hospital, complete diagnostic imaging, and begin treatment within 4.5 hours. In remote areas or resource-limited countries, this treatment window is virtually impossible to meet.

**2. The bleeding risk is too high**

Patients receiving tPA have a **2-6%** chance of symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage (sICH).

This may not seem high, but in practical terms: if you're an emergency physician, for every 20 patients you treat with tPA, one may experience ICH leading to death or further deterioration.

This risk causes many physicians to prefer "non-intervention"—because there's a fundamental difference between harm caused by the disease itself versus harm caused by the physician's treatment decision.

**3. Result: Global utilization below 5%**

Over the past 30 years, pharmaceutical companies have invested billions of dollars attempting to develop better thrombolytic agents.

All have failed.

Either efficacy was insufficient, or bleeding risk remained too high. The clinical trial graveyard for stroke drugs is filled with former "rising stars."

## Why LT3001 May Be Different

LT3001 is an innovative short peptide drug developed by Taiwan's Lumosa Therapeutics. Its core mechanism is fundamentally different from tPA:

| Dimension | tPA | LT3001 |
|-----------|-----|--------|
| Activation pathway | Direct injection of thrombolytic enzyme (exogenous) | Utilizes the body's own thrombolytic system (endogenous) |
| Scope of action | Systemic (dissolves everywhere) | Local (targets only the clot) |
| Treatment window | 4.5 hours | **24 hours** |
| ICH rate | 2-6% | **0%** (N=297) |

This isn't incremental improvement—it represents a fundamental mechanistic difference.

More importantly, this difference has been preliminarily validated in **clinical data**:

- **Phase 2 trial (N=297)**: 0 cases of symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage
- **Animal studies**: Head-to-head against tPA showed superior results (greater infarct reduction at equivalent doses, lower bleeding)
- **FDA response**: Positive feedback from Type C meeting, agreement to proceed to Phase 3

## My Conclusion (The Answer First)

After extensive research, my core conclusion is:

> **LT3001 has the potential to become the most significant breakthrough in stroke treatment in 30 years.**

Three pillars support this conclusion:

**Pillar One: Mechanistic Innovation**

LT3001 is not an "improved tPA"—it represents an entirely different thrombolytic pathway. It works by redistributing the body's endogenous plasminogen, enabling the body's own thrombolytic system to "overclock" at the clot site. This explains why it can achieve both "greater efficacy" and "greater safety" simultaneously.

**Pillar Two: Safety Data**

297 patients, 0% symptomatic intracranial hemorrhage. This is not statistical fluctuation—it's mechanistic evidence. If Phase 3 maintains this safety profile, LT3001 will redefine the risk-benefit equation for stroke treatment.

**Pillar Three: Regulatory Signals**

FDA's positive response to the Type C meeting indicates that regulators see beyond the surface-level numbers. Their agreement to focus Phase 3 on "moderate-to-severe + disabling" patients is itself an indirect endorsement of the Phase 2 subgroup analysis results.
